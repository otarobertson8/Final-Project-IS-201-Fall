Pickleball is a fast-growing paddle sport that combines elements of tennis, badminton, and table tennis.

It is played on a small, rectangular court with a paddle, a plastic perforated ball, and a low net, making it accessible and easy to set up. The game is known for being easy to learn, fun for all ages, and social, yet it can also be highly competitive at advanced levels. Pickleball emphasizes strategy, quick reflexes, and precise placement rather than sheer power, allowing players of varying skill levels to enjoy long rallies and exciting gameplay. Its combination of simplicity and depth has made it one of the fastest-growing sports in the world, attracting players from beginners to professional athletes.

HOW TO PLAY PICKLEBALL

  • Serving Rules
    • The serve must be underhand.
    • Serve diagonally into the opposite service box.
    • The serve must land past the kitchen line.
    • Call the score before serving.
    • Only one serve attempt is allowed.
  • Bounce Rules
    • The receiving team must let the serve bounce before hitting it.
    • The serving team must let the return bounce before hitting it (double-bounce rule).
    • After the double-bounce rule, you may hit the ball in the air (volley).
  • Kitchen (Non-Volley Zone) Rules
    • You may not volley while standing in the kitchen.
    • Your momentum cannot carry you into the kitchen after a volley.
    • You may step in the kitchen at any time as long as you are not volleying.
  • Scoring Rules
    • Only the serving team can score points.
    • Games are usually played to 11, win by 2.
    • Players switch sides with their partner only when their team scores a point.
  • Faults
    • Hitting the ball out of bounds.
    • Hitting the ball into the net.
    • Volleying in the kitchen.
    • Missing the ball on a serve.
    • Letting the ball bounce twice on your side.
    • Stepping into the kitchen after a volley.
  • General Play Rules
    • A ball landing on any line is in (except a serve hitting the kitchen line is out).
    • You may step outside the court to return a ball.
    • Play continues until a fault is committed.

HISTORY OF PICKLEBALL

Pickleball was invented in 1965 on Bainbridge Island, Washington, by Joel Pritchard, Bill Bell, and Barney McCallum as a family-friendly backyard game. They combined elements of tennis, badminton, and table tennis using a lowered net, paddles, and a perforated plastic ball. The game’s popularity grew quickly because it was easy to learn, required less space than tennis, and was suitable for all ages. In the 1970s and 1980s, community centers and schools began adding pickleball courts, helping it spread across the United States. Today, pickleball is one of the fastest-growing sports worldwide, with professional tours, international competitions, and a rapidly expanding player base.

PRO PICKLEBALL SCENE

The professional pickleball scene has grown rapidly over the past decade, transforming from a niche hobby into a competitive, widely recognized sport. Major tours like the Professional Pickleball Association (PPA), Association of Pickleball Professionals (APP), and the Major League Pickleball (MLP) host tournaments across the United States and internationally, offering significant prize money and media coverage. Top players compete in singles, doubles, and mixed doubles, with some achieving “triple crown” victories by winning all three events at the same tournament. The sport attracts athletes from diverse backgrounds, including former tennis, badminton, and table tennis players, contributing to high-level, fast-paced competition. Sponsorships, streaming platforms, and social media have elevated the visibility of pros, helping to grow fanbases and inspire a new generation of players. With increasing professionalism, structured rankings, and growing global participation, the pickleball pro scene is quickly establishing itself as a mainstream competitive sport.
